A Step- By-Step Guide to Improving Your Credit Score

Your credit score is an indicator of how responsibly you manage credit. A higher credit score means companies see you as a lower risk, so you’re more likely to be approved for higher credit limits at lower interest rates, and have access to more offers.

This is why it’s important to improve and keep your credit score high. Read on to know how you can do that in a few easy-to-follow steps.

Focus on Collection Accounts First

While letting an account get so late it goes to a collections agency is not ideal, there’s good news. Credit scoring algorithms may now reward you more than they did in the past for paying these accounts in full. The key is to get everything in writing – make sure to request a letter stating that you’ve made the payment in full and that they will update your credit report to reflect this.

Use Services Like Experian Boost

While paying your bills on time will help push your credit score upward, it can take time. Free services like Experian Boost, which allows you to add utility and telecom bills to your credit file, can help you out here.

Pay Off Debts Close to The Credit Limit

A debt utilization ratio over 50% will have a definite negative impact on a credit score, as will a maxed-out card.

Request a Higher Credit Line if Possible

Or open a new line of credit. This way you’ll owe the same amount of money but against a higher credit line – thus improving your credit-to-debt ratio. Note that this is only a good option if your credit score is in the high 600s or low 700s, and you’re not a foolish spender.

Reach out To Your Credit Card Company

According to a CreditCards.com report, about two-thirds of American credit cardholders who asked their card issuers for a lower interest rate and nearly nine out of ten who requested that their late payment fees be waived were successful.

Put a Utility Service in Your Name

Call your providers and find out who reports to the credit bureaus. Another way to rebuild credit is to report your living expenses, like rent, to the credit bureaus.

If you still feel like you need help managing your credit, avoid for-profit “credit repair” companies and reach out to non-profit counseling options like the NFCC.
